# Principles-Based Framework for Aboriginal Governance

## Section I: The Nation-to-Nation Principle

### Historical Foundation
The Royal Proclamation of 1763 stands as a foundational constitutional document that recognizes the inherent sovereignty of Indigenous nations. Paragraph 4 of the Proclamation explicitly states that Indigenous lands cannot be taken without their consent, affirming: 

> "And whereas it is just and reasonable, and essential to our Interest, and the Security of our Colonies, that the several Nations or Tribes of Indians with whom We are connected, and who live under our Protection, should not be molested or disturbed in the Possession of such Parts of Our Dominions and Territories as, not having been ceded to or purchased by Us, are reserved to them, or any of them, as their Hunting Grounds."

This establishes a framework of mutual respect and formal negotiation between the Crown and Indigenous nations, grounded in the principle of free, prior, and informed consent.

### Constitutional Significance
This principle of a "Nation-to-Nation" relationship is not merely a historical artifact; it is a living constitutional principle that informs the interpretation of Section 35 of the *Constitution Act, 1982*. The Supreme Court of Canada has repeatedly affirmed that the Crown's relationship with Indigenous peoples is grounded in the honor of the Crown, which derives from the Proclamation's recognition of Indigenous nations as distinct political entities.

### Contrast with the Indian Act
Unlike the Indian Act, which imposes a paternalistic, trustee‑based relationship, the Nation‑to‑Nation principle envisions a partnership of equals. The Indian Act's regime of unilateral control over band governance, land management, and personal status stands in direct opposition to the Proclamation's guarantee of Indigenous self‑determination.

### Modern Application
A principles‑based governance framework must therefore restore the Nation‑to‑Nation relationship as the cornerstone of all future agreements. This means:
1. Recognizing Indigenous nations as constitutionally‑protected governments with inherent jurisdiction.
2. Ensuring that any negotiation or agreement proceeds on a government‑to‑government basis.
3. Replacing the Indian Act's discretionary powers with treaties or modern agreements that reflect the free, prior, and informed consent of the Indigenous nation.
